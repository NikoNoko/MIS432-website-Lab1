Section 01 — The Method

How does it find matches?

Every drug in our database affects at least one biological target — a protein or enzyme inside the body. Some of those same targets are also involved in diseases the drug has never been tested on. When a drug and a disease share a target, that is a repurposing hypothesis.

We score each match by counting how many targets connect the drug to the disease. More shared targets means a stronger, more believable suggestion.

D
STEP 01

Store the drugs

Each drug is stored with its approved uses — diseases it already treats. This prevents the system from suggesting something the drug already does.

T
STEP 02

Map the targets

Each drug is linked to the biological targets it interacts with — proteins and enzymes it switches on or off inside the body.

D
STEP 03

Link targets to diseases

Research has shown that certain targets play a role in certain diseases. These links are stored in a third dictionary.

S
STEP 04

Score the matches

The engine walks from drug to target to disease. Every shared target adds 1 to the score. Higher score = stronger evidence for repurposing.

Section 02 — The Structure

A tiny knowledge graph

The three Python dictionaries together form a knowledge graph — a web of connected facts. Nodes are drugs, targets, and diseases. Edges are the "affects" and "linked to" relationships stored in the dictionaries.

The repurposing engine simply finds two-hop paths: Drug → Target → Disease. A path that avoids already-approved uses is a candidate.
